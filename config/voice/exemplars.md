# VisQuanta Voice Exemplars

These 8 paragraphs are the ground truth for the voice transform step (spec §5d).
All pulled from real published posts at visquanta.com. When the drafter's voice
transform stage runs, these are loaded as few-shot examples and the instruction is:
**"Rewrite the factual paragraphs below in the voice of these examples. Do not
change facts. Do not add facts. Do not remove citations."**

Source post: `visquanta-site/content/blog/ai-roi-for-dealerships.md` (2026-03-31)

---

## Exemplar 1 — The direct Bottom Line opener

> **The Bottom Line:** 76% of dealers are increasing AI budgets in 2026. But most still can't answer "what's the ROI?" Here's what the data actually shows after 90 days - the wins, the misses, and a simple formula to calculate it yourself.

**Why this works:** Opens with one provocative stat, immediately names the gap (dealers can't answer ROI), then tells the reader exactly what's coming. No "in today's fast-paced world" windup. Reader knows in 30 seconds whether to keep reading.

---

## Exemplar 2 — The "cut through the BS" stance

> Let's cut through the vendor pitch decks. Here's what third-party research shows about dealership AI performance after 90 days of implementation:

**Why this works:** One-sentence paragraph that earns the reader's trust by naming the thing they're skeptical of (vendor pitch decks) and promising the opposite (third-party research). This is how VisQuanta sounds when it's about to drop a table of numbers.

---

## Exemplar 3 — Using a stat to reveal a gap

> But here's the disconnect: while enthusiasm is high, clarity on ROI remains low.

**Why this works:** "Here's the disconnect" is a VisQuanta signature transition. Sets up contrast in six words. Never uses "however" or "on the other hand" — those are dead words.

---

## Exemplar 4 — Calling out what matters from a data dump

> That last data point matters: not a single dealership reported that AI hurt their revenue.

**Why this works:** After listing percentages, the writer stops and tells the reader which number to care about and why. This is the anti-dump: never list stats without highlighting the one that changes the argument.

## Exemplar 5 — The industry-specific contrarian beat

> The automotive industry has shifted from asking "should we use AI?" to "which AI should we buy?" According to Spyne's 2026 U.S. Automotive Market Sentiment Report — based on a survey of nearly 1,200 dealership leaders — 76% plan to increase their AI budgets this year.

**Why this works:** Frames the cultural moment in dealer-insider language ("should we" → "which should we buy"), then anchors it with a specific source and sample size (1,200 leaders, not "thousands"). Voice marker: dashes for parenthetical facts, never commas.

---

## Exemplar 6 — Listing failure modes without hedging

> Out-of-the-box AI implementations are failing at dealerships. Dealers who assume AI will just plug in and solve everything tend to see poor results.

**Why this works:** Declarative. No "may struggle" or "could encounter challenges." Failure is stated as fact. Dealers can smell hedging from a mile away.

---

## Exemplar 7 — The "you already know X, here's Y" structure

> AI is a coverage and consistency layer around your team, not a replacement for skilled people. The strongest model is hybrid: AI handles speed and consistency, humans handle complexity and relationships.

**Why this works:** Two-sentence paragraph. First sentence corrects a misconception (AI replaces people — no), second sentence gives the nuanced take (hybrid, with a specific division of labor). This is how VisQuanta sounds when it's being pedagogical without talking down.

---

## Exemplar 8 — The terse closer

> Start with one workflow. Measure everything. Expand what works.

**Why this works:** Three five-word sentences. No caveats, no "in conclusion," no "ultimately." This is how the brand ends posts. Three imperative verbs, period.

## Voice summary — what to preserve in every voice transform

1. **Direct openers.** Lead with the most provocative stat or the sharpest observation. Never windup.
2. **Specific numbers + specific sources.** Not "many dealers" — "74% of dealers (Cox Automotive)." Always cite inline.
3. **Dealer-insider vocabulary.** BDC, rooftops, CDJR, SRP, VDP, ROs — never "car dealership business" or "automotive retail sector."
4. **Dashes, not commas.** For parenthetical facts.
5. **Short paragraphs.** 1–4 sentences. Paragraphs are rhythm, not containers.
6. **Declarative failure.** Call out what's broken directly — "is failing," "doesn't work," "missed the point."
7. **Call out what matters.** After data, one-sentence paragraph saying which number changes the argument.
8. **Terse closers.** 2–4 sentences, imperative verbs, no windup.
9. **Never use the banned.txt phrases.** Zero tolerance.
10. **No moralizing.** Don't tell dealers what they "should" feel — tell them what the data says and let them decide.
